Using Artificial Intelligence Responsibly

A look at how AI is changing the way we think, and how to use it without losing the skills that make us human.

Introduction

What Is AI, and Why Does It Matter?

This project was about looking into how AI and computers impact the way we think. Do they help us think better, or do they do all the thinking for us? Based on the research, the answer is somewhere in the middle, but AI is starting to replace the thinking humans do.

One study published in 2025 tested 666 individuals of all ages. The more people used AI tools, the worse they scored on critical thinking tests. The youngest age group, 17 to 25, used the most AI and scored the lowest. Researchers call this "cognitive offloading," which is when humans let another mind (like an AI system) think for them. The researchers didn't think AI was making people less smart, just that their brains were getting out of practice at thinking for themselves.

The Research

What Can Go Wrong

Cognitive offloading and the Google Effect

The MDPI study found that the more people relied on AI, the lower their critical thinking scores. It's not that AI makes you dumber, it's that you stop building the mental muscle when you stop using it. Researchers compare this to the "Google Effect," where people forget information they know they can look up online. AI takes that even further by letting people skip the thinking part entirely. Another study found that moderate AI use can actually be helpful, but overusing it stops your brain from learning how to do things on its own.

Hallucination

AI doesn't actually know anything. It guesses what the answer should be based on what it has seen before. It will often make up information that sounds legitimate but is wrong, like fake quotes or wrong dates. Always double check important information against a reliable source before trusting it.

What this could mean long term

If people keep using AI the way they do now, it won't just affect grades. It could leave the next generation of students weak in the exact skills colleges and employers want. The fix isn't to avoid AI, it's to use it in a way that maximizes its benefits.

"By far, the greatest danger of artificial intelligence is that people conclude too early that they understand it."
Eliezer Yudkowsky, AI researcher

Better Use

Using AI to Think Better, Not Less

Ask it to explain, not just answer

Instead of asking AI for the answer, ask it to explain how to get the answer step by step. That makes you actually engage with the reasoning instead of copying a result. Once you understand the reasoning, you can apply it to new problems.

Use it to challenge your thinking

Write out your argument, then ask AI to argue against it: "what's the best argument against this?" That's what good writers and lawyers do. The goal isn't for AI to win, it's to make your argument stronger.

✓ Learning-first prompts

  • "Explain this concept like I'm new to it"
  • "What's wrong with my reasoning here?"
  • "Give me 3 practice questions on this topic"
  • "What's the counterargument to what I just wrote?"

✗ Shortcut prompts

  • "Write my essay for me"
  • "Give me the answer to question 4"
  • "Summarize this book so I don't have to read it"
  • "What should I think about this topic?"

Practical Rules

How to Use AI Safely

✓ Do

  • Verify AI facts with a real source
  • Be honest when AI helped with your work
  • Use AI as a starting point, not a final answer
  • Question outputs that seem too confident
  • Keep personal info out of AI chats

✗ Don't

  • Submit AI-written work as fully your own
  • Share passwords, SSNs, or financial info with AI
  • Treat AI opinions as objective truth
  • Spread AI content without checking it first
  • Let AI replace your own thinking

Conclusion

The Bottom Line

AI tools are not good or bad, they are simply tools like the internet and social media. Research shows that using AI too much can hurt your ability to think for yourself but using the tool correctly can actually help you to learn. Think of AI tools like any other tool in the world; they are simply tools to aid your efforts but you must never let them take away your individuality.

"The machine does not control us. We control the machine. And it is our duty to use it wisely."
Max Tegmark, MIT physicist and AI researcher
